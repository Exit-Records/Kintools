# Kin Catalog — Copy Review

Updated descriptions for all 25 tools. The aim: match the Kin voice (direct, human, no hype), stay clear for non-technical users, and let the “Replaces” line do the heavy lifting on context.

Notes on general patterns across the current copy:

- Some descriptions lean slightly technical (“chromatic tuner”, “GCEA standard”, “Flesch-Kincaid readability scores”) where plainer language would land better for most people
- A few tools over-explain features that are better discovered than listed
- The strongest descriptions already on the site (Quiet Cycle, Thought Loop, Kin Nest) set the tone — warm, honest, slightly wry. The weaker ones read more like feature specs
- “Nothing sent anywhere” / “Nothing stored” / “Nothing leaves your device” appears in various forms — worth standardising to one or two phrases used consistently

-----

## KIN-001 — Ukulele Tuner

**Current:**
Chromatic tuner for ukulele. GCEA standard and other tunings. Listens through your mic and shows you which way to turn.

**Suggested:**
Tune your ukulele using your phone’s microphone. Standard and alternate tunings. Shows you which way to turn the peg. That’s it.

**Why:** “Chromatic” and “GCEA standard” mean nothing to most ukulele beginners — and beginners are the people most likely reaching for a free tuner. Keep it in their language.

## General recommendations

**Standardise the privacy line.** Across the catalog, you currently have:

- “Everything happens in your browser, nothing sent anywhere”
- “Nothing stored”
- “Nothing leaves your device”
- “Nothing saved, nothing sent”
- “Your data stays on your device”
- “Nothing stored, nothing sent”
- “Nothing saved server-side”

Pick two or three of these and use them consistently. Suggested standard phrases:

1. **“Nothing leaves your device.”** — for tools that store data locally (Flashcards, Mood Slider, Quiet Cycle, Nest)
1. **“Nothing stored, nothing sent.”** — for tools that don’t save anything at all (Decision Flip, QR Code, Recipe Scaler)
1. **“Everything happens in your browser.”** — as a warm alternative for either, when you want variety

**Lead with the verb, not the noun.** The strongest descriptions start with what you *do* (“Tune your ukulele”, “Tap along to anything”, “Work out which size is cheaper”). The weaker ones start with what the tool *is* (“Chromatic tuner for ukulele”, “Full-screen countdown timer”). Shift toward action where possible.

**Features vs benefits.** Catalog cards should describe what the tool does for you, not what it contains. Feature lists belong on the app page itself. The card is the sleeve notes, not the track listing.
